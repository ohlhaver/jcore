God save the NHS

Posted by:
Bagehot
Categories:
The NHS

THERE'S a lot of ignorance on both sides of the transatlantic spat over health-care models and the NHS, in which both Gordon Brown and David Cameron are now embroiled. In Britain there are exaggerated ideas about Americans being turned away from hospitals to die in the gutter. And in America there seems to be a silly notion that having a state-funded health system such as the NHS is somehow tantamount to socialism.

Britain is not a socialist nation. It is much less left-wing, in terms of economic philosophy and welfare provision, than many other European countries. But it does have a deep attachment to the NHS, deriving in part from a folk memory of what it was like, for many people, to fall sick in Britain before the service was introduced in 1948. This attachment is an anomaly in Britain's political consciousness rather than evidence of some more pervasive ideology.

And, as it happens, it is turning out to be a rather sensible quirk. As all developed countries struggle to contain health-care costs, the single-payer system is regarded by many as one of the most plausible ways to control them. The British reliance on GPs, who keep most patients away from the more expensive hospitals, is an important strength. Universal provision is also a good answer to some of the problems likely to arise from advanced genetic testing, which in some places may create a rump of uninsurable malcontents.

This is not to say that the NHS is perfect by any means (though Americans ought to bear in mind that Britons are able to supplement it with private insurance of their own). There is too much political meddling and not enough real competition within the system. Other countries, notably France, probably do better. But on the usual measures of efficiency, equity and health outcomes, most sensible people would conclude that, if you had to choose between them, the British model is preferable to America's.

That still leaves the socialism tag, and some Americans' allergy to the perceived infringement of liberty involved in state organisation of health care. But there is a good case to be made that health-care provision falls within the state's legitimate function, in a liberal society, as a guarantor of choice. Without reasonable health-care security, citizens' freedom to purse fulfilling lives and happiness is sorely constrained.

Dyett1133 wrote:

August 17, 2009 21:42

It is really tiring to read commentary from British, American and Canadian commentators who are totally unable to think outside of the "medical insurance" box. Here in Mexico, and in Thailand where I have also lived, fee-for-service medicine is widely available, inexpensive and much more personal than I was used to in the US. Why? There are virtually NO third-party payers!

When you introduce third-party payers--doesn't matter how many such payers there are--you immediately lose any vestige of cost control. The person receiving the services has no incentive to control costs because they are not paying for it--at least not directly. The party doing the paying lacks the ability to control costs--doing so often costs more than simply paying off. The person receiving payment has every incentive to pad the services or their costs, knowing that no one will really check up on them (this is especially true of Medicare in the US).

Furthermore, if government is the third-party payer the situation is even worse. In the first place, care will invariably be rationed. Secondly, those with the duty to control costs (if indeed there are any such functionaries) ultimately draw upon an almost unlimited pot of money (i.e., taxes collected at gunpoint), so they are unlikely to really do their jobs. Thirdly, government will always attach politically-driven strings to services, requiring inordinate record keeping, certifications, etc., all of which add costs.

Here in Mexico, my doctor typically spends oven an hour with me in an office visit. We can discuss anything of concern. The cost: about $15 US! I retain my own medical records, X-rays, etc., so my doctor has only a small office--no receptionist, no records clerk, no medical assistant. Hospitals, imaging centers, etc. are competitive, and they post their prices for all to see. They also have sales! (When I needed an MRI my doctor called around and found a nearby hospital that was having a sale on MRIs that day. Another time, when I needed a simple X-ray, she made a call and a man showed up at her office with a portable machine in less than ten minutes.) And, as one might guess, medical services are not rationed. When I needed an operation, it was scheduled entirely at MY convenience.

May I also add that, like most of the world, there is no requirement for prescriptions for most drugs.

Get third-party payers, and most especially the government, out of health care and watch costs drop and service increase. Let's think outside the box for a change!

Healthcare costs matter much more than cars trains or planes for several rather obvious reasons.

1st off a significant portion of transportation spending is discretionary. You can choose to drive instead of fly or stay home instead of vacation in Aruba, or buy a cheap car instead of a $35000 SUV.

2nd, healthcare costs when it does hit someone tends to be catastrophic and unplanned. Your blogger mentioned someone having to mortgage their house. Such events are rare in the UK but sadly frequent in the USA.

3rd, medical insurance be it private or public tends to suffer from what economists term "the tragedy of the commons". If you don't put rules in place what happens is that nobody feels ownership and takes as much as they can. The point being the demands for healthcare are basically infinite, the resources are not. Society has to decide at what level and how equitably to fund the demands.
