Taliban Besiege Kabul

Afghan Elections Targeted by Attacks on Palace, NATO Convoy

By MATTHEW ROSENBERG, ANAND GOPAL and PETER SPIEGEL

KABUL -- Taliban insurgents shelled and bombed the Afghan capital in separate assaults that hit the presidential palace and killed eight people in an attack on a NATO convoy, in a show of intimidation that threatened to damage Thursday's presidential vote and unsettle the coalition supporting U.S. strategy here.

Around 7 a.m., mortar shells whistled over the center of Kabul before slamming into the grounds of the presidential palace. In the afternoon, the shock wave was felt across the city from the bombing of the North Atlantic Treaty Organization convoy, which left vehicles smoldering into the evening near a British military base on a major route out of the capital.

View Interactive

The Kabul attacks make five in the past week for which the Taliban claimed responsibility, killing 15 people and injuring 144. The series has broadened the militant campaign from its rural strongholds, potentially giving voters fear that even polling places in the heavily guarded capital might not be safe. "They have been stockpiling weapons and bombs. They have people here," said a Western civilian security official in Kabul who advises the Afghan government on security matters. "They are just waiting to move at the right time."

Early Wednesday, armed gunmen stormed a branch of the Pashtani Bank in central Kabul, a top city police official said. But the official said police haven't been able to ascertain whether the gunmen, holed up in the bank, were Taliban insurgents or a criminal gang.

The Associated Press reported that a Taliban spokesman said 20 armed suicide attackers wearing explosive vests had entered the city and five of them were battling police.

Security officials here asserted a turning point in the militants' strategy, confirmed by a local Taliban commander in an interview with The Wall Street Journal. The commander said militants are now focused on intimidating voters by attacking polling stations before they can cast their ballots.

In an effort to protect its 7,000 polling places, the government is trying to keep the locations secret until Election Day. But at least four were attacked with assault weapons and rocket-propelled grenades in two rural provinces south of the capital on Monday and Tuesday, according to local and Western officials. In Wardak province, teachers at schools that are potential polling centers have started to receive death threats, said two lawmakers from the province.

The presidential election Thursday, the second since the fall of the Taliban in 2001, is seen as critical to the country's stability. If violence keeps large numbers of people from the polls, it could diminish the legitimacy of the government, making it harder for Afghanistan and the U.S. and its allies to overcome the insurgents.

In a sign of the potential effectiveness of militants' attacks, Afghanistan's Foreign Affairs Ministry on Tuesday ordered all news organizations to avoid "broadcasting any incidence of violence" between 6 a.m. and 8 p.m. on Election Day. The order aims "to ensure the wide participation of the Afghan people," a statement said. The Interior Ministry also ordered journalists to stay away from the scene of any Election Day attacks.

The mounting violence -- and recent rising death toll among U.S. and allied forces -- also threatens to further undermine shaky support for the Afghanistan campaign in NATO countries that have lost soldiers in recent months, including Canada, Germany and France. It has been an especially divisive issue in Britain, where a public outcry followed the loss of 15 soldiers in 11 days in July, the deadliest month for allied forces since the start of the war.

State of the Nation

Key indicators in Afghanistan.

In the U.S., officials have been gauging public and congressional sentiment toward the buildup of U.S. forces.

Taliban fighters are exploiting a U.S. decision to continue having local government forces take the lead in establishing election security, as in previous Afghan and Iraqi elections. The idea is to avoid a U.S. or NATO military presence giving any appearance of foreign meddling in the vote.

NATO commanders acknowledge that Afghan security forces remain overstretched and undertrained. Army training centers sped up their graduation of several units so that the Afghan army could be at 92,000 troops. But NATO officials said allowing Afghan units to handle on-the-ground security was essential.

"I think it's very important for the Afghan national security forces to be seen by the Afghan people as the lead agency in execution of security for their elections," said Australian Army Brig. Gen. Damian Cantwell, head of NATO's election task force.

Another reason for the strategy: There are not enough foreign troops in the country to provide primary security in all of the polling centers.

The security situation could prevent nearly two million of 17 million registered voters from going to the polls, Gen. Cantwell said. He said recent U.S. and NATO offensives have opened up areas to voters who just a few weeks ago were unlikely to participate. But he acknowledged that "pockets" of some southern provinces will remain too dangerous for voters -- as will even some areas of eastern Afghanistan where the U.S. has better control.

Despite the plan to keep foreign troops in the background, senior U.S. officials said they were closely studying this week's attacks, particularly the Taliban's repeated use of rockets and mortars against targets in and around Kabul. The officials noted that though the attacks caused few casualties they attracted immense public attention, and acknowledged that similar attacks on Election Day could significantly affect voter turnout.

A high-ranking American military official said the U.S. and allies planned to devote more resources on Election Day to securing areas on the outskirts of polling places that the Taliban could use to launch the weapons. The official said the U.S. would also fly unmanned aerial drones over specific "high-risk" polling sites in eastern and southern Afghanistan to look for suspicious vehicles that could contain Taliban fighters or car bombs.

Afghan officials have had some success in combating the Taliban's moves. In July, authorities captured a suspected suicide bomber who was believed to be planning to target government buildings in Kabul.

But outside Kabul, the government has little influence beyond provincial capitals, especially in many parts of the south and east dominated by Pashtuns, who form Afghanistan's largest ethnic group and the core of the Taliban movement.

In Wardak, for instance, roughly 60% of polling stations may remain closed, according to an internal government document seen by The Wall Street Journal. "There is going to be almost no voting in the Pashtun areas of my province," said one lawmaker there, Roshanak Wardak, in an interview.

An extremely low turnout in Pashtun areas would allow the militants to press their case that the government doesn't represent the largest ethnic group.

That could represent a particular threat to President Hamid Karzai, a Pashtun who presides over a fragile coalition of ethnic leaders. Mr. Karzai is fending off a challenge from his former foreign minister, Abdullah Abdullah, among other candidates. Despite having a Pashtun father, Dr. Abdullah is more identified with his mother's Tajik ethnic group and is likely to benefit if Pashtun voters stay away from the polls.

Tuesday's Kabul attacks offer an ominous signal of the government's security difficulty. Attacks on Kabul have been rare, but the Taliban now appear intent on creating the impression they can strike anywhere in the country.

"The blast blew out the windows of our house, and I could smell gunpowder in the air. I saw shrapnel everywhere," said Samiullah, 26 years old, a crane operator who uses only a single name.

Among the people killed in the suicide bombing were one NATO soldier and two Afghans working for the United Nations, officials said, along with five other civilians who were nearby. NATO didn't specify the dead soldier's nationality. No casualties were reported in the mortar attack, said a government spokesman.

The Taliban earlier claimed responsibility for a suicide bombing Saturday in front of the Kabul headquarters of NATO's Afghanistan task force and two rocket attacks in the capital Thursday.

—Yochi J. Dreazen contributed to this article.
